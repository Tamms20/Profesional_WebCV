About Me

Bridging Science
& Global Health

I am a Neuroscience graduate with research experience in experimental neurobiology, where I studied the effects of a hypercaloric–hypoprotein perinatal environment on hippocampal formation. This work shaped my interest in neurodevelopment and in understanding how early-life and environmental factors influence brain structure and function. I am currently focused on translational approaches that connect experimental findings with public health perspectives.

In the long term, I aim to contribute as a bridge between scientific research and public health, participating in strategies that promote maternal and child health and help preserve optimal neurodevelopment across populations.

Academic Work

Experimental Neurobiology

My academic work has focused on experimental neurobiology, particularly on the effects of early-life nutritional environments on brain development. During my undergraduate training, I investigated how a hypercaloric-hypoprotein perinatal environment affects hippocampal formation, integrating behavioral, histological, and analytical approaches.

This experience allowed me to develop strong skills in experimental design, animal models, histological techniques, and data interpretation, while strengthening my interest in understanding neurodevelopment from a biological and integrative perspective.

Research Vision

Translational Neuroscience

My research vision is centered on understanding how early-life and environmental factors shape neurodevelopment and long-term brain function. I am particularly interested in translational approaches that allow experimental findings to inform strategies beyond the laboratory.

In the long term, I aim to contribute to bridging neuroscience research with public health, supporting the development of interventions and policies that promote maternal and child health and help preserve optimal neurodevelopment at the population level.
